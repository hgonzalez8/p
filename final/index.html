Summary

For the Final Project for CS184, our group wanted to extend upon the illumination and raytracing methods built previously in Homework 3. This project centers around using the pathtracer from homework 3, and implementing 2 different light transport features: A Volumetric Scattering algorithm and a Bidirectional Path Tracer (BDPT). The volumetric system adds a participating medium model that supports absorption, scattering, transmittance, anisotropic phase functions, and direct lighting from scattering events inside fog or haze. The Bidirectional path tracing pipeline builds paths from both the camera and the light, connects compatible vertices, and uses multiple importance sampling weights to combine different light transport strategies. These additions expand the renderer from surface-only global illumination toward more realistic and expressive light transport, including fog, beams of light, reflective caustic-like effects, colored illumination, and atmospheric scattering.

Technical Approach

Volumetric Scattering

In a basic volumetric scattering algorithm, we sample a ray of light by starting from the camera, and calculating the probability of intersecting with medium before hitting a surface. If it hits a particle we use the medium's properties to determine the scattering of the light and how much light gets absorbed along the way. We also calculate the amount of light that passes through to surfaces or reflected, and to terminate rays over many bounces. These changes change the amount of light that reaches the camera as well as the distribution of the light across a scene.

Medium Struct

The first thing we implemented was a medium struct that defined the properties of the medium between surfaces. Our Medium Struct uses different coefficients for absorption, scattering, and extinction. These properties define how likely light is to intersect with particles, how they scatter, and the amount of light that gets absorbed.
https://www.astro.uvic.ca/~tatum/stellatm/atm5.pdf

Beer-Lambert Law

We use the Beer-Lambert Law to determine Transmittance, the amount of light that passes through a medium, based on the extinction coefficient. This affects the intensity of the medium and how clear surrounding objects can be seen.
https://pages.mtu.edu/~scarn/teaching/GE4250/transmission_lecture.pdf

Distance Sampling

We adjusted the sampling by using a cumulative density function based on the extinction coefficient to determine how likely a ray would intersect with the medium. If the distance returned by this function was greater than the distance to a surface then it does not intersect with the medium.

Albedo

We use an albedo function which uses the scattering and extinction coefficients as the probability of an extinction event, when the ray is no longer scattered.

Henyey-Greenstein Phase Function

One improvement to standard volumetric scattering we implemented was a Henyey-Greenstein Phase Function. Real light scattering is non-uniform, and we simulate this by calculating the probability distribution of light scatter from an incoming angle to an outgoing angle.
https://www.oceanopticsbook.info/view/scattering/level-2/the-henyey-greenstein-phase-function

Other Approaches

In this source, they use a concept called Ray Marching which approximates and accumulates the light changes caused by particles. Instead, we use a CDF in our distance sampling function to quickly calculate this change based on the distance sampled and the coefficients of the medium. This allows us to efficiently calculate light, but the tradeoff is being unable to show heterogeneous changes. https://cseweb.ucsd.edu/classes/sp17/cse168-a/CSE168_14_Volumetric.pdf

Bidirectional Path Tracing Approach

The main focus of our project is to make an analysis between Unidirectional and Bidirectional Path Tracing in rendering with Volumetric Scattering. We shoot two rays one starting from the camera and the other from a light source and create vertices at the points they register an intersection at. We try to connect the vertices to each other from both rays and if unblocked a valid light path is created. With the inclusion of a medium in the scene this creates an extremely large number of positions in which light can reflect and scatter. This makes it a lot harder for rays to bounce and intersect with a light source before terminating. https://cs.dartmouth.edu/~wjarosz/publications/georgiev13joint-small.pdf

Path Vertex Struct

The BDPT stores its paths as vectors of a special vertex struct. This is the core building block of the following functions for the BDPT, and stores the following information for constructing medium vertices, surface vertices, and light-source vertices. A vertex represents a point along a BDPT path and will store information about its world-space position, surface normal (if applicable), accumulated path throughput, and direction towards the previous vertex. It will also track:
  • Whether the vertex is -- a volume scattering event or emissive
  • Surface material pointer
  • Whether the interaction is specular/delta
  • placeholders for path probability bookkeeping (pdfFwd, pdfBwd)

Camera Subpathing

To generate camera subpaths, we take the input camera ray and repeatedly sample either a medium scattering event or a surface hit. For each detected bounce, it will intersect the ray with the BVH and sample a medium distance. If the medium event occurs before the surface hit, it will create a medium vertex and sample a new phase-function direction. Otherwise, it will create a surface vertex, sample the BSDF, update throughput, and continue.

Light Subpathing

For generating light subpaths, it will randomly select one scene light and parse that through a sampling function that handles point lights, area light, infinite hemisphere lights, directional lights, and spotlights. The sampled light creates an initial light-source vertex, then traces forward similarly to the camera subpath. At each bounce, it may also add either a medium or surface vertex, but will also update the throughput.

Connecting Vertices

Afterwards, we will connect all surface vertices together. It will search for a pair of non-specular, non light, non-medium surface vertices and compute the direction of the light-side vertex to the camera-side vertex to check whether they are visible to each other. If visible, it will draw a shadow ray between them and return the medium transmittance along the segment.

Problems Encountered

A problem we encountered were noisy images during the initial testing of our BDPT algorithm. The original BDPT applied an arbitrary brightness scale to its returned contribution term, which would produce unstable image brightness in scenes. It also does not account for how likely each path construction strategy was, leading to scenes with fairly unrealistic lighting.
One idea we had was to mitigate this issue was to replace the arbitrary brightness term with Multiple Importance Sampling (MIS), which assigns each sampling strategy a fraction of the contribution based on its probability of generating the same transport path: \( \text{weight} = \frac{\text{current_strategy_pdf}}{\sum \text{all_strategy_pdfs}} \)
This was intended to reduce overcounting between different BDPT connection strategies and downweight poor sampling strategies, improving variance compared to a fixed manual scale.
Another issue was using the BDPT alongside the Volumetric Scatterer. The original BDPT hardcodes its surface-to-surface geometry term: \( G = \frac{\cos L \cdot \cos C}{\text{distance}^2} \)
When used with the volumetric scatterer which assumes all vertices are surfaces with valid normals, we end up with bright scenes with no fog. To handle this, our modified BDPT adds medium-aware geometry and scattering helpers. Surface vertices use BSDFs and cosine terms, while medium vertices use the medium phase function and a geometry term appropriate for points in a participating medium. http://luthuli.cs.uiuc.edu/~daf/courses/rendering/papers/lafortune96rendering.pdf

Lessons Learned

We learned how to realistically simulate light transport through a medium using physics and various methods to sample and distribute light. Through this we gained a better understanding of how light rays are scattered and how we could manipulate them to add caustics such as fog, haze, and dust. Using the Henyey-Greenstein phase function gave our pathing more depth making our renders better. Implementing BDPT taught us the sensitivity of parameters and the importance of weights to mitigate noise and uneven sampling.
